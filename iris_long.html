Longitudinal Performance of Iris Recognition in Children: Time Intervals up to Six years

Authors: Priyanka Das, Naveenkumar G Venkataswamy, Laura Holsopple, Masudul H Imtiaz, Michael Schuckers, Stephanie Schuckers

Conference: 2023 11th International Workshop on Biometrics and Forensics (IWBF)

DOI: 10.1109/IWBF57495.2023.10157312

Objective

This study investigates the long-term stability of **iris recognition performance in children** over a period of **6.5 years**. The research aims to determine whether **aging affects iris recognition accuracy**, which is critical for biometric systems used in applications such as **national identity programs, border security, and child protection services**.

Methodology

  • Dataset: Collected iris images from 230 children over **10 sessions**, spanning **78 months (6.5 years)**.
  • Biometric System Used: **Iris Guard IG-AD100 Dual Iris Camera** for high-quality iris capture.
  • Performance Evaluation: Analyzed False Non-Match Rate (FNMR) and **match score (MS) trends** over time.
  • Statistical Analysis: Applied **Linear Mixed Effects Regression Model (LMER)** to assess the impact of factors such as:
    • Enrollment age
    • Pupil dilation
    • Difference in dilation between paired samples
    • Time interval between enrollments

Results

  • No significant impact of aging on iris recognition performance for children aged **4 to 17 years** over 6.5 years.
  • FNMR remained consistently **low (below 0.8%)**, confirming the **stability of iris biometrics in children**.
  • Dilation variation was the most influential factor in match score fluctuations, but did not affect overall recognition reliability.
  • All **false rejection cases** were due to **poor-quality images**, not aging-related changes in the iris.

Conclusion

The study provides **strong evidence** that **iris biometrics remain stable in children over time**, reinforcing their suitability for **long-term biometric applications**. The results suggest that **iris recognition technology can reliably be used for children without frequent re-enrollment requirements**. Future research will focus on refining **capture techniques and minimizing quality-related errors**.
